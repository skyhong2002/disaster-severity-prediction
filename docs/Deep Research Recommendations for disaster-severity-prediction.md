# Deep Research Recommendations for disaster-severity-prediction

## Executive summary

For your repository and competition setting, the highest-probability path to a **better private leaderboard** is not a wholesale move away from gradient-boosted trees. The strongest evidence from modern tabular benchmarks is that tree ensembles still dominate **medium-sized, heterogeneous tabular datasets**, especially when data are not massive, features are irregular, and robustness matters more than architectural novelty. That matches your task unusually well: a panel of regions, a moderate 91-day history window, mixed weather and score-history features, missing values, direct multi-horizon regression, and MAE as the competition metric. Your current choice of direct horizon models is also methodologically defensible for multi-step forecasting. citeturn40view0turn11search1turn11search17

The next model family I would implement is **CatBoost**, not because it is guaranteed to beat LightGBM or XGBoost on every fold, but because it is one of the most credible sources of **diversity plus robustness** in tabular data. It supports MAE optimization, handles missing values and categorical variables natively, and its ordered boosting is explicitly designed to reduce target leakage / prediction shift; CatBoost’s own documentation also notes that ordered boosting often gives better quality on smaller datasets. In a time-series repository, however, there is one crucial caveat: you should preserve chronological order by using `has_time=True` and an explicit timestamp or already time-sorted data, because the default random permutations are not what you want in a leakage-sensitive sequential problem. citeturn32view0turn13search18turn12search12turn13search3

The other big opportunity is **validation design**, not just model family. Published evaluations of forecasting model selection consistently show that preserving time order is essential, and that in real-world non-stationary settings, out-of-sample temporal splits tend to give more realistic error estimates than generic cross-validation. In your case, the most plausible reason for “very strong local MAE” not matching the public leaderboard is not that the leaderboard is wrong, but that the local validation is probably too optimistic because of overlapping windows, insufficient embargo between train/validation origins, or feature construction that is technically legal but distributionally too close to the validation block. A stronger validation protocol will probably improve your private-LB rank more than another round of ad hoc hyperparameter tuning. citeturn8search5turn8search6turn39view1

If you want one neural model for completeness or for the final report, implement **one** rather than five. The best candidate is **N-HiTS** or an exogenous **N-BEATSx/N-HiTS-style** global model, not TFT, DeepAR, or a generic LSTM/GRU/TCN sweep. TFT is explicitly a large model and PyTorch Forecasting’s own tutorial says it performs much better with more data; DeepAR is fundamentally designed for probabilistic forecasting across many related series; both are reasonable research baselines, but neither is the best near-term private-LB optimization bet for your exact repository. citeturn33view0turn41search0turn41search1turn34view0turn34view1turn41search2

## Recommended validation protocol

Your main model-selection criterion should be **rolling-origin, expanding-window validation over the most recent part of the training period**, with **all regions present in every fold**, and with an **embargo gap of at least the full forecast horizon** between the last training origin and the first validation origin. This preserves time order, mirrors deployment, and is aligned with the forecasting literature that finds temporally ordered out-of-sample evaluation more realistic for non-stationary real-world series. Scikit-learn’s `TimeSeriesSplit` exists precisely because ordinary cross-validation would train on future data and evaluate on the past, and it supports a `gap` parameter to exclude samples just before the test block. citeturn8search5turn39view1turn39view0

For your task, I would define a fold on **forecast origins**, not on raw rows. If an origin at time \(t\) predicts the next 5 weeks, then any training origin whose label window overlaps the validation label window should be excluded. In practice, that means an embargo gap \(G\) of **at least 35 days**, and often **42 days** is safer if your target is weekly aligned or there is any reporting lag. That specific gap length is an inference from your horizon, but the logic is standard: use temporal order, avoid train/validation contamination, and ensure the model is evaluated on genuinely later origins. citeturn39view0turn39view1turn11search17

A strong practical protocol for this competition is:

- Use **four to six recent rolling folds**.
- In each fold, train on all regions up to cutoff \(c_i - G\), validate on all regions for origins inside a later validation block.
- Keep the training window **expanding**, not shrinking, unless concept drift is severe.
- Report **overall MAE**, **horizon-wise MAE** for weeks 1–5, and the **standard deviation across folds**.
- Keep one **final untouched pseudo-private holdout block** at the very end, used only after feature/model choices are frozen. citeturn8search5turn39view1turn14search0

Region-grouped holdouts are useful only as a **stress test**, not as the main selection rule, unless the hidden competition test may contain **unseen regions**. If the private test uses the same regions at later times, a “hold out entire regions” protocol is too pessimistic and can push you toward the wrong model family. If you want an extra robustness check, do one secondary experiment where you hold out a few climatologically distinct regions or region clusters, but do not let that replace chronological validation. citeturn14search0turn39view1

What typically makes local MAE look too good is not subtle math; it is almost always one of a few operational mistakes: shuffled or random splits, target-derived rolling features without proper shifting, train/validation origins whose target windows overlap, time-normalization fitted on all data, leakage through target encoding, or repeated tuning against the public leaderboard. Competition theory and competition-scale meta-analysis both show that repeated reuse of leaderboard feedback leads to measurable overfitting. In your repository, the correct stance is: **trust the stronger temporal CV more than the public LB**, and use the public LB only as a coarse diagnostic or tie-breaker when two models are already close on leakage-safe CV. citeturn15search0turn15search4turn15search17

## Feature engineering recommendations

For drought severity forecasting, the feature story should be **persistence + multi-scale hydroclimate memory + seasonal context + region heterogeneity**. Drought indices are inherently multi-scalar: SPI measures precipitation deviations from climatology over a selected timescale, SPEI extends this idea by incorporating evapotranspiration / climatic water balance, and EDDI focuses on anomalous atmospheric evaporative demand. NOAA and Drought.gov also emphasize that drought monitoring combines indicators such as precipitation, temperature, soil moisture, and related hydrometeorological variables. That is exactly why your feature engineering should lean so heavily on **lagged, rolling, cumulative, anomaly, and trend features** at multiple time scales rather than only raw daily values. citeturn6search4turn26search1turn25search0turn25search1turn25search12turn6search6

The most useful feature groups for your repository are these:

| Feature family | What to compute | Why it should help |
|---|---|---|
| **Historical drought persistence** | `score_lag_1, 7, 14, 21, 28, 35, 42, 56, 70, 84, 91`; rolling means / mins / maxes / stds of the historical score over 7/14/28/56/84 days; exponentially weighted means; recent slope of score over 14/28 days. Always compute per region with a strict backward shift before rolling. | Drought evolves slowly and persistence is often the strongest short-horizon baseline. Drought forecasting studies and operational drought systems repeatedly rely on antecedent drought state, soil moisture, and recent hydroclimate anomalies as core predictors. citeturn20search11turn7search0turn25search4 |
| **Rolling weather summaries** | For each weather variable, use rolling mean / std / min / max over 3, 7, 14, 28, 56, 84, and 91 days; add last-value and last-week-change features. | Lagged and rolling features are standard and effective for ML forecasting on temporal/tabular problems because they convert sequential dependence into supervised features. citeturn24search2turn24search0turn24search13 |
| **Cumulative precipitation and deficits** | Accumulated precipitation over 7/14/30/60/90 days; precipitation deficit relative to region/day-of-year normals over the same windows; dry-spell counters such as “days since meaningful rain” if that concept exists in your data. | SPI-style drought information is explicitly based on precipitation departures from climatology across chosen accumulation windows. citeturn6search4turn6search0 |
| **Temperature and evaporative-demand anomalies** | Mean/max temperature anomalies over recent windows relative to region/day-of-year climatology; optional heat degree accumulations; if humidity, wind, and radiation exist, create evaporative-demand proxies or EDDI-inspired anomalies. | SPEI and EDDI both exist because drought severity is not only about rainfall shortage; atmospheric demand and temperature anomalies matter, especially for developing and intensifying drought. citeturn26search1turn25search0turn25search1turn25search17 |
| **Trend and change features** | Linear slope over the last 14/28/56 days for precipitation, temperature, humidity, VPD-like variables, and the score itself; week-over-week changes; distance from recent extrema. | Drought onset and recovery are about **direction**, not only level. Multi-step forecasting literature and practice benefit from summary signals of recent local dynamics. citeturn24search2turn24search13 |
| **Seasonality and calendar** | Day-of-year sine/cosine encodings; week-of-year; month; optional periodic splines if you want a richer seasonal basis. | Cyclical feature engineering is a standard way to encode recurring seasonal structure without hard boundaries, and drought risk is strongly seasonal in many regions. citeturn24search3turn24search7 |
| **Region-level static context** | Region ID, climate zone, elevation band, long-run train-only climatology, region-wise average historical severity by season, region-wise weather normals. | Global models improve when region heterogeneity is represented explicitly. CatBoost is particularly attractive here because it can use region-like categorical features directly. citeturn13search18turn12search13turn20search11 |
| **Spatial aggregates** | If adjacency or coordinates exist, add neighbor-mean recent severity, neighbor precipitation deficit, neighbor temperature anomaly, and cluster-level drought summaries using only information available at or before the origin date. | Drought has spatial structure; recent drought work and operational systems increasingly leverage spatial relationships or neighboring units. A simple neighbor aggregate is a much lower-risk first step than a graph neural model. citeturn20search11turn20search13turn20search14 |

In implementation terms, a **gap-safe rule** should govern every target-derived feature: sort by `region, date`, then for any score-history feature at origin \(t\), compute it from values strictly **before or at the forecast origin**, never from any date inside the predicted 5-week window. In pandas terms, the safe pattern is generally `groupby(region).shift(1)` before any rolling operation on the target history when the row itself corresponds to a forecast origin. That is the cleanest defense against accidental leakage in a final-project repository. citeturn39view1turn8search5

One additional recommendation that is easy to underappreciate: create **climatology-normalized anomalies per region and day-of-year**. Raw precipitation or temperature levels can vary dramatically by region; drought forecasting generally cares about whether the recent period is *unusually dry* or *unusually hot* for that region and season. Using departures from train-only climatology often improves both transfer across regions and private-LB robustness because it reduces the burden on the model to rediscover each region’s seasonal baseline from scratch. This is strongly motivated by the logic of SPI, SPEI, and EDDI, all of which are anomaly-based drought indicators. citeturn6search4turn26search1turn25search0

## Ensemble recommendations

Your current LightGBM/XGBoost weighted ensemble is directionally correct. Heterogeneous forecast combinations are a recurring winner in forecasting because they reduce the risk of selecting one brittle “best” model. But the same literature also warns that **over-optimized ensemble weights are often less reliable than simple combinations**, because weight estimation itself is noisy. That matters a lot in competition settings where you only have a handful of temporal folds. citeturn22search9turn22search1turn22search6

For this repository, the default ensemble should be **prediction averaging**, not rank averaging. Your target has a meaningful absolute scale and the competition metric is MAE, so calibrated point values matter. Rank averaging discards scale information and is usually more appropriate when the downstream metric is rank-based or when different models live on incomparable score scales. Here, if all models predict the same severity score, rank averaging should be treated as a robustness experiment, not the default final method. That is an inference from the point-forecast combination literature and from your metric choice. citeturn22search9turn22search1

The most practical robust ensemble is:

- Base learners: **LightGBM + XGBoost + CatBoost**
- One model per horizon, or one ensemble weight vector per horizon
- Non-negative weights summing to one
- Weights chosen from **rolling-origin out-of-fold** predictions, but shrunk toward equal weights if the estimated optimum is unstable across folds. citeturn22search9turn22search6turn32view0turn36view0

Horizon-specific weights are more important than many teams think. Week-1 predictions are usually more persistence-driven, while weeks 4–5 benefit more from cumulative deficits, seasonal context, and slower climate memory. Different model families often have different error profiles by horizon, so a single global weight vector can be suboptimal even if the overall MAE looks good. This is also consistent with multi-horizon forecast comparison work, which emphasizes evaluating models across the full horizon path rather than only as a single aggregated score. citeturn11search8

Validation-based stacking is worth trying **only after** you have strong rolling-origin OOF predictions. If you do stack, use something conservative such as **Ridge regression on OOF predictions**, optionally one stacker per horizon. Stacking has a solid theoretical pedigree, but in small or weakly separated validation sets it can easily turn into another optimism trap. In your competition setting, a convex average of three strong boosted-tree models will usually be a safer final submission strategy than a highly parameterized meta-learner. citeturn22search12turn22search0turn22search9

A robustness-first submission policy would be:

1. Pick candidate models using rolling-origin CV only.
2. Keep the public leaderboard for coarse monitoring.
3. Finalize weights from OOF CV and a pseudo-private holdout.
4. Submit **few** final blends rather than continuously chasing public-LB noise. citeturn15search0turn15search4turn15search17

## Risks and leakage warnings

The biggest leakage risk in your repository is not blatant future-feature usage; it is **validation mismatch that behaves like leakage**. With a 91-day encoder and a 5-week forecast horizon, nearby origins share a large portion of their feature history. If train and validation origins are too close, you can get unrealistically good local MAE even when there is no literal future column leak. Adding an embargo gap between training and validation origins is the cleanest fix. citeturn39view0turn8search5

The second major risk is **target-derived feature leakage**. Common examples are rolling means of drought score that accidentally include the current origin or future values, region statistics computed over the full dataset rather than train-only history, and stage-two features built from predictions that were themselves trained on rows too close in time to the validation block. Every target-history feature should be constructed by strict shifting, and every encoding/statistic that depends on the target should be fit inside each training fold only. citeturn39view1turn8search6

The third risk is **CatBoost chronology misuse**. CatBoost’s ordered boosting is a strength, but in time-series data it must use the actual temporal order. The official docs are explicit that `has_time=True` tells CatBoost to use the input order / timestamp and skip random permutations in the ordered-transform and tree-building stages. If you add CatBoost without this safeguard, you may get misleading validation behavior. citeturn13search3turn13search1

The fourth risk is **public leaderboard overfitting**. The theory literature on competition leaderboards, and competition-scale empirical analyses of Kaggle-like settings, both show that repeated leaderboard reuse creates genuine overfitting pressure. This does not mean the public LB is useless; it means you should not use tiny public-LB deltas to choose between many highly correlated experiments. In your repository, models that are stable across rolling folds, horizons, and a pseudo-private holdout are more likely to survive the private leaderboard shake-up. citeturn15search0turn15search4turn15search17

The final risk is **reporting only one validation number**. For a multi-horizon disaster forecast, one aggregated MAE can hide a model that is excellent at week 1 and poor at weeks 4–5, or a model that is fragile in only one late fold. For both scientific honesty and competition robustness, report at least: overall MAE, horizon-wise MAE, fold mean and standard deviation, and the pseudo-private holdout score. That will make your final report much more convincing and make your model-selection process harder to fool. citeturn11search8turn8search5
